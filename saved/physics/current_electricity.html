Chapter: Current Electricity, Electrical Energy, and Power

1. Introduction to Current Electricity

Electricity plays a vital role in our daily lives. It powers our homes, industries, and transport systems. In this chapter, we explore the flow of electric charges (current), the energy involved, and how power is consumed in electric circuits.

2. Electric Current

Definition:

Electric current is the flow of electric charge (usually electrons) through a conductor.

Formula:

I=QtI = \frac{Q}{t}I=tQ​

Where:

  • III = Current (Ampere, A)

  • QQQ = Charge (Coulomb, C)

  • ttt = Time (seconds, s)

SI Unit: Ampere (A)

1 Ampere = 1 Coulomb/second

Types of Electric Current:

  • Direct Current (DC): Flows in one direction. (e.g., from a battery)

  • Alternating Current (AC): Changes direction periodically. (e.g., from the mains supply)

3. Electric Potential and Potential Difference

Electric Potential:

The work done to bring a unit positive charge from infinity to a point in an electric field.

Potential Difference (Voltage):

The work done in moving a unit charge from one point to another.

Formula:

V=WQV = \frac{W}{Q}V=QW​

Where:

  • VVV = Potential Difference (Volt, V)

  • WWW = Work done (Joule, J)

  • QQQ = Charge (Coulomb, C)

4. Ohm's Law

Statement:
The current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across its ends, provided the temperature remains constant.

V∝I⇒V=IRV \propto I \Rightarrow V = IRV∝I⇒V=IR

Where:

  • VVV = Potential difference (V)

  • III = Current (A)

  • RRR = Resistance (Ohm, Ω\OmegaΩ)

Resistance:

The opposition offered by a conductor to the flow of current.

Factors Affecting Resistance:

  • Length of the conductor (R∝LR \propto LR∝L)

  • Cross-sectional area (R∝1AR \propto \frac{1}{A}R∝A1​)

  • Nature of material

  • Temperature

6. Heating Effect of Current (Joule’s Law)

When current flows through a resistor, electrical energy is converted into heat.

Formula:

H=I2RtH = I^2RtH=I2Rt

Where:

  • HHH = Heat produced (Joules)

  • III = Current (A)

  • RRR = Resistance (Ohms)

  • ttt = Time (s)

Applications:

  • Electric heaters

  • Fuses

  • Electric bulbs

7. Electrical Energy and Power

Electrical Energy:

The work done by electric current in an electric circuit.

E=VIt=I2Rt=V2tRE = VIt = I^2Rt = \frac{V^2t}{R}E=VIt=I2Rt=RV2t​

  • Unit: Joule (J)

  • Practical unit: Kilowatt-hour (kWh)
    1 kWh =3.6×106 J1 \text{ kWh } = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ J}1 kWh =3.6×106 J

Electric Power:

The rate at which electric energy is consumed or produced.

P=Et=VI=I2R=V2RP = \frac{E}{t} = VI = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R}P=tE​=VI=I2R=RV2​

  • SI Unit: Watt (W)

  • 1 kW =1000 W1 \text{ kW } = 1000 \text{ W}1 kW =1000 W

8. Commercial Unit of Energy

  • 1 kWh = Energy consumed by a device of 1000 W running for 1 hour.

  • Electricity bills are calculated based on energy consumption in kWh (units).
