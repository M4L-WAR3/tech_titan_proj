Chapter 1: Force

1.1 Introduction to Force

In everyday life, we push, pull, lift, or throw objects. All these actions involve the use of force. Force is a fundamental concept in Physics, affecting motion and producing changes in an object’s shape or state of rest.

Definition of Force

Force is a physical quantity that changes or tends to change the state of rest or motion of a body or changes its shape.

SI Unit: Newton (N)
CGS Unit: Dyne
1 N = 10⁵ dyne

Force is a Vector Quantity

Force has both magnitude and direction, making it a vector quantity.

1.2 Effects of Force

A force can:

  • Change the state of rest or motion of a body

  • Change the speed of a moving body

  • Change the direction of motion

  • Change the shape or size of a body

Examples:

  • Kicking a football changes its speed and direction.

  • Squeezing a rubber ball changes its shape.

1.3 Types of Forces

(A) Contact Forces

These forces act only when objects are in physical contact.

  1. Muscular Force: Exerted by muscles (e.g., lifting a box).

  2. Frictional Force: Opposes motion between two surfaces in contact.

(B) Non-Contact Forces

Forces that act from a distance.

  1. Gravitational Force: Attraction between two masses.

  2. Electrostatic Force: Between charged bodies.

  3. Magnetic Force: Between magnets and magnetic materials.

1.4 Resultant Force

When more than one force acts on a body, their vector sum is called the resultant force.

  • If forces act in the same direction, add them.

  • If in opposite directions, subtract the smaller from the larger.

1.5 Newton's First Law of Motion (Law of Inertia)

Statement:
A body remains in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless an external force acts on it.

Inertia: The property of a body by virtue of which it resists a change in its state of motion.

Examples:

  • A book on a table stays at rest unless pushed.

  • Passengers jerk forward in a stopping bus due to inertia of motion.

1.6 Newton’s Second Law of Motion

Statement:
The rate of change of momentum of a body is directly proportional to the applied force and takes place in the direction of the force.

Mathematical Form:

F=maF = maF=ma

Where:
FFF = Force in Newton (N)
mmm = Mass in kg
aaa = Acceleration in m/s²

1 Newton Definition:

1 Newton is the force that produces an acceleration of 1 m/s² in a mass of 1 kg.

1.7 Newton’s Third Law of Motion

Statement:
To every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Examples:

  • Recoil of a gun

  • Walking: Foot pushes back, ground pushes forward

1.8 Friction

Friction is a contact force that resists motion between surfaces.

Advantages:

  • Helps us walk

  • Brakes in vehicles

Disadvantages:

  • Causes wear and tear

  • Wastes energy as heat

Reducing Friction:

  • Use of lubricants

  • Using wheels or rollers

  • Streamlining objects

1.9 Pressure and Force

Pressure is the force applied per unit area.

Pressure=ForceArea\text{Pressure} = \frac{\text{Force}}{\text{Area}}Pressure=AreaForce​

SI Unit of Pressure: Pascal (Pa)
1 Pa = 1 N/m²

Applications:

  • Sharp knives exert more pressure

  • Broad tires reduce pressure on sand

1.11 Summary

  • Force can change motion or shape of an object.

  • It is a vector quantity with SI unit Newton.

  • Newton’s Laws describe how forces affect motion.

  • Friction is a contact force opposing motion.

  • Pressure depends on force and area.
